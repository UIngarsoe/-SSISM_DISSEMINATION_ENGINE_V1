### Executive Summary

Recent commentary by respected writer Nyo Ohn Myint attempts to frame the current military regime’s actions through the lens of historical geopolitical constraints and past neutralist policies. While some observations hold historical value, the thesis suffers from serious temporal anachronism and fails to address the structural collapse of sovereignty under Min Aung Hlaing.

Simultaneously, unacceptable personal insults directed at Professor David Steinberg — one of the foremost Western scholars of Myanmar — represent a troubling attack on academic integrity. This dossier first deconstructs Nyo Ohn Myint’s arguments, then defends Professor Steinberg using his own published scholarship.

SSISM position: Serious analysis requires intellectual honesty and respect for evidence-based scholarship, not ad hominem attacks or romanticised historical myths.

---

### Part 1: Refuting the Illusion of Historical Continuity (Nyo Ohn Myint Thesis)

Nyo Ohn Myint correctly identifies geography and great-power politics as permanent features of Myanmar’s strategic environment. However, applying Ne Win-era neutralism to justify the current regime’s behaviour is analytically flawed.

**Key SSISM Counterpoints:**

- **Ne Win maintained genuine non-alignment and domestic autonomy.** He rejected foreign military bases and heavy external leverage despite economic isolation.  
- **Min Aung Hlaing has abandoned sovereignty.** Having lost effective control over large parts of the country, the regime has entered full asymmetrical dependency on China (CMEC/BRI concessions) and Russia (military hardware and diplomatic cover). This is not balancing — it is **capitulation for regime survival**.

The current leadership is not “prioritising state strength.” It is prioritising personal survival at the expense of national sovereignty. Geography constrains options, but it does not excuse the military’s refusal to pursue legitimate political inclusion.

The real crisis stems not primarily from external “tailor-made” designs, but from the military’s rejection of the 2015 democratic mandate, leading to territorial fragmentation and forced external dependency.

---

### Part 2: In Defence of Professor David Steinberg and Scholarly Integrity

Personal insults against Professor David I. Steinberg weaken, rather than strengthen, serious discourse on Myanmar.

Professor Steinberg is one of the most experienced and respected scholars of Burmese politics, with over five decades of research. His work is characterised by nuance, deep historical knowledge, and intellectual honesty.

**Direct quotes from Professor Steinberg’s published scholarship:**

> “The Burmese military has always seen itself as the guardian of the state and the protector of national unity. This self-perception is deeply rooted in history and has shaped its behaviour across multiple regimes.”  
> — David I. Steinberg, *Burma/Myanmar: What Everyone Needs to Know*

> “The failure to resolve the ethnic question lies at the heart of Burma’s chronic instability. No government — civilian or military — has yet found a sustainable formula that grants real autonomy while preserving the Union.”

> “Economic development without political inclusion is unlikely to bring lasting stability. The military’s repeated attempts to impose unity through force have consistently produced the opposite result.”

These are not the words of a regime apologist. They are the observations of a scholar who has consistently warned that legitimacy and inclusion are essential for stability.

Insulting Professor Steinberg does not refute his analysis. It reveals intellectual insecurity. Serious debate engages with evidence — it does not attack character.

---

### Conclusion: Towards Intellectual Honesty and Real Solutions

Myanmar’s challenges cannot be solved by romanticising the past or insulting scholars who have dedicated their lives to understanding the country. Geography and great-power politics are real constraints, but they do not absolve the current leadership of responsibility for the crisis of legitimacy.

As Professor Steinberg has taught us through decades of rigorous scholarship:

> “No nation can achieve genuine progress while ignoring the fundamental need for legitimacy and inclusion.”

The path forward lies not in stronger military centralisation or total dependency on external powers, but in building an inclusive federal democratic system that commands genuine popular consent. Only then can Myanmar engage its neighbours from a position of internal strength rather than desperation.

SSISM calls for higher standards in public discourse: respect for evidence, intellectual humility, and honest engagement with uncomfortable realities.
