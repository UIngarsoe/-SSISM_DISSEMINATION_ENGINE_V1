In Mon State, located in southeastern Myanmar bordering Thailand, the dynamics are complex. Historically, the region has seen tensions between the majority Mon ethnic group and the Bamar-dominated central government. While not as insurgent-heavy as northern Shan or western Rakhine states, Mon State has become a hotspot for post-coup resistance. Groups like Ye Bilu operate as underground forces, blending local grievances with the national anti-junta struggle. The junta relies on proxy militias like Pyu Saw Htee to maintain control, especially in areas where regular troops are stretched thin.

The SAC's strategy involves arming and supporting these militias to counter PDFs and ethnic armed organizations (EAOs). ## Who Are the Pyu Saw Htee Militias?

The Pyu Saw Htee are pro-military paramilitary groups that have proliferated since the coup. Named after a legendary Burmese king known for slaying enemies of the ancient Bagan Kingdom, these militias symbolize ultra-nationalist defense of Buddhism and the state. Their conceptual origins trace back to the 1950s, during the post-independence communist insurgencies.

Post-2021, Pyu Saw Htee groups have emerged as village-based militias, often comprising USDP supporters, Buddhist nationalists, and army veterans. They receive weapons, training, and logistical support from the junta to offset manpower shortages and gather local intelligence. In Sagaing Region, leaked documents reveal the junta arming over 77 such groups with more than 2,000 weapons by early 2022.

In Mon State, Pyu Saw Htee operate in rural areas like Ye Township, where they man checkpoints, extort travelers, and suppress dissent. Lin Oo, the targeted leader in Aye Ywar, oversees a camp with nearly 70 fighters, collecting "gate fees" and "soap money" from locals. These militias are accused of atrocities, including village burnings and civilian killings, often in retaliation for resistance attacks.
